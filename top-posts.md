### Post #1 — 4/3/2026 (5,509 imp · 104 eng · 1.89%) — TOP REACH

```
I bought a micro SaaS for $4,000. It was making $500/mo.

6 months later, it was doing $5,000 MRR. I eventually exited for 6 figures.

Here's the playbook I used, and why this is the most underrated path into business ownership right now:

1/ The math is simple. A SaaS making $500/mo = $6K/yr. At a 2x multiple, that's a $12K asset.
You can buy it for <$10K cash upfront. The market misprices these assets every day.

2/ You're not just buying revenue. You're buying a competitive advantage you can exploit — a terrible landing page, zero marketing, weak target customer. If you can fix what they couldn't, the upside is yours.

3/ Growth doesn't need to be complicated. We changed Sourcely's target customers from broke students to college professors. LTV jumped overnight. Then we layered in SEO + influencer marketing.

4/ Structure protects you. 60-70% upfront, rest deferred and tied to revenue targets. Always use an APA and escrow. Never wire money to a stranger on a handshake.

5/ The exit takes care of itself. Recurring revenue + predictable cash flow = higher multiples.

$5K MRR × 12 × 2.5x = $150K exit. From a $4K buy-in.

Still early in sharing the full framework publicly. Not every deal works this cleanly — I've also overpaid and learned expensive lessons. But the model works if you pick the right asset and stay disciplined on diligence.

Save this if you're thinking about your first acquisition.

Comment "playbook" and I'll DM you the full case study.
```

**Archetype:** PLAYBOOK REVEAL — buy→grow→exit story with numbered tactical breakdown
**Hook structure:** Two-line receipt → expanded receipt. Line 1 = shocking input ("$4K for $500/mo"). Line 2 = shocking output ("6 figures"). No setup, no throat-clearing.
**Structural spine:**
- Hook: stat-pair (buy price + MRR)
- 1-line outcome update
- Transition: "Here's the playbook I used..."
- 5 numbered sections (1/ 2/ 3/), each opens with a bold principle then proof/mechanism
- Real talk admission ("I've also overpaid and learned expensive lessons")
- Explicit save/comment dual-CTA
**Voice tells winning here:** napkin math made explicit ($5K × 12 × 2.5x = $150K), specific sector example (Sourcely + professor pivot), "the market misprices these assets every day" as a quotable line

- Headline hook (no verb, short)
- Labeled "Hot take:" + prediction
- "Let me explain." — mini-cliffhanger
- Historical analogy with named source (Irving Grousbeck, 1984, Stanford)

### Flop to analyze — 3/20/2026 (1,794 imp · 15 eng · 0.84%) ❌

Decent reach, weak engagement — reading this tells us what didn't land even when the algorithm gave it distribution.

```
I sent a cold DM to a founder who had no plans to sell.

His app was 30 days old.
Doing about $25K a month.
Posting progress like someone settling in for a long grind.

Most people would scroll. I didn't.

My analyst had been combing Twitter for founders building health and wellness apps in public. His profile came up. I checked the app, felt a small spark of potential, and asked my analyst to reach out.

He didn't trust us at first. Fair enough.

If you're building in public and a random stranger asks if you want to sell, it sounds shady. Competitor fishing? Time-waster? Fake buyer? He'd heard enough horror stories to keep his guard up.

So I got on a call myself and laid everything out.
Who the buyer was.
What they owned.
Why this app made sense.
What a real deal might look like.

No pressure. Just clarity.

That changed the tone. Suspicion faded. Curiosity took over.

The buyer, One Degree Labs, wasn't browsing deals for fun. Their team were runners. Serious ones. They had tried every major app and felt this one approached the problem differently. When buyers already believe in the product, you don't have to drag momentum out of them.

But here's the part that tested me.

The founder wanted a six-figure exit for a one-month-old app.

Most buyers would've laughed and walked.

I couldn't. My job was to find a structure that respected his ambition without exposing the buyer to reckless risk.

So we built a layered deal.

Less than half was paid upfront.
The rest came through structured payouts, a salary component, and equity rolled over.

He still had upside if the app grew.
The buyer got breathing room on cash exposure.

And honestly, revenue wasn't the real story.

No Android version yet.
No paid acquisition.
No influencer pushes.
No localisation.

Plenty left to try.

What the buyer really saw was unused potential and a clear roadmap. They weren't buying the performance. They were buying headroom.

Due diligence moved quickly because everything was clean. Financials organised. Product logic documented. Roadmap written out. The buyer could open any folder and understand what was happening without needing a guided tour.

I've seen strong businesses fall apart in diligence because the backend was chaotic. This one wasn't.

The part I keep thinking about, though, is how simple the sourcing was.

No brokers. No listings.
Just tweets.
An analyst searched. I read a thread, and I sent a DM.

That was enough to start the conversation that led to an acquisition.

Building in public did what no pitch deck could.

Right now, distribution matters more than code. Products can be rebuilt quickly. An audience, early traction, and clear execution plans are harder to copy.

If you care about how real exits happen, explore Pocket Fund and tune into the podcast for deeper insights.
```

**What failed (why 0.84% engagement despite 1,794 impressions):**
- **No hook punchline.** Opens with a setup ("I sent a cold DM...") but never delivers the reveal/stat in the first 2 lines. Compare to winners which front-load numbers in line 1.
- **Too long for the payload.** The post runs 400+ words to land at "building in public > pitch decks." The insight isn't proportional to the read time. Readers who scrolled past ...see more never got a payoff.
- **Mid-post promo** ("One Degree Labs" named as buyer) reads like a case-study write-up, not a thesis post. Feels like *content marketing* rather than a *take*.
- **No numbered structure** — it's one continuous story. Winners use 1/ 2/ 3/ or bulleted arrows so readers can skim and still get value.
- **Soft CTA** ("explore Pocket Fund and tune into the podcast") — passive, multi-channel, no single action to take. Contrast: Post #1 says "Comment 'playbook'".
- **No debate trigger.** Nothing to agree/disagree with. No contrarian claim, no hot take, no reframe. Story without stance.
- **Ends on the buyer's benefit, not the reader's.** The reader gets no "here's what this means for you" takeaway.

**Lesson for future:** case-study posts work only when (a) the first 2 lines contain the shocking number, and (b) a contrarian claim or reframe rides the story. Without those two ingredients, even great content flops.

## What the flop taught us (anti-patterns)

The 3/20 post got 1,794 impressions but only 0.84% engagement. Three structural failures we must avoid:

1. **No stat in the first 2 lines.** Every winner front-loads a number. The flop opens with a setup sentence.
2. **No debate trigger.** No contrarian claim, no reframe, no enemy. Readers had nothing to react to.
3. **Promo-shaped close.** "Tune into the podcast and explore Pocket Fund" is a passive, multi-channel CTA. Winners use one specific action (comment a keyword, swipe, save).

Case-study-as-narrative works on Twitter. On LinkedIn it needs a thesis hook or it flops.
